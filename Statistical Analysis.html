Truncal Analgesia for Midline Laparotomy

A Bayesian Systematic Review and Meta Anlaysis, Metaregression and Trial Sequential Analysis

Author

Dr Benjamin Moran

Published

July 11, 2023

1 Introduction

This is an explanation of the Bayesian analysis of the meta analysis examining the effects of truncal analgesia (e.g. TAP, Rectus Sheath, Perperitoneal, Erector Spinae, Paravertebral Blocks) compared to placebo, thoracic epidural and wound catheters for midline laparotomies in elective surgery.

2 The Meta Analysis Model

As we anticipate a high level of heterogeneity due to differing patient cohorts, different pathologies and small studies, we will use a random-effects (hierarchical) model. The Bayesian meta-analytic (hierarchical) model is given by:

\[ \begin{align*} y_i & \sim Normal(\theta_i, \sigma_i^2) \tag{Likelihood} \\ \theta_i & \sim Normal(\mu, \tau^2)\\ \mu & = \alpha_{subgroup[k]}\\ \\ \alpha & \sim \operatorname{Normal}(0, 1) \tag{Priors} \\ \tau & \sim \operatorname{Half-Cauchy}(0, 0.5) \\ \end{align*} \]

where \(y_i\) is the observed mean difference in the dynamic numerical rating scale (NRS) of truncal blocks versus non-truncal regional analgesia for midline laparotomy, and \(\sigma_i^2\) is the known sampling variance in the study \(i\). Each study, \(i\) has it’s own distribution with a mean effect, \(\theta_i\). \(\theta_i\) is from a normal distribution with a mean effect \(\mu\) and variance \(\tau^2\) (which represents the between-study heterogeneity). The mean effect, \(\mu\), is predicted by a linear regression, where each subgroup \(k\) (Control, Placebo, Thoracic Epidural, Wound Catheter) has it’s own mean effect, given by \(\alpha\). It should be noted that \(\alpha_{subgroup[k]}\) is the intercept in the model.

Within a Bayesian framework, the above likelihood is combined with priors of the unknown parameters \(\alpha\) and \(\tau\).

3 Choice of Priors

3.0.1 Prior for Mean Difference in Numerical Rating Scale (NRS)

The numerical rating scale (NRS) is an 11-point ordinal scale that has bounds at [0,10]. Despite this, a large majority of pain studies report the mean and standard deviation in the effect estimates of the intervention. Hence, it was not possible to convert the effects to median with interquartile range, and a decision was made to convert all values to mean and standard deviation. For this reason, the mean difference was the effect measure chosen for this meta analysis.

The mean difference in interventional pain studies can potentially have no effect (mean difference = 0), or can result in total resolution of pain (mean difference = 10). It is unlikley that a pain intervention will result in improvement in NRS more than 5/10. However, it should also be noted that if a pain intervention has no effect on acute pain, a pain score may increase due to continued or worsening nociception, resulting in a positive mean difference. Again,this is unlikely to result in a mean difference of -10, and is less likely to exceed -5. Hence, we will use a weakly informative prior for \(\alpha\), ie \(Normal(0,1)\), which will cover the parameter space for the mean differences.

This gives:

\[\begin{align*} \alpha & \sim \operatorname{Normal}(0, 1) \ \end{align*}\]

3.0.2 Prior for the Variance

The variance, \(\tau^2\), is non-negative, even though it can be close to 0 (Note- a fixed effects model is one where \(\tau^2 = 0\)). A commonly used distribution is the Half-Cauchy distribution, which is a special case of the positive half of the Cauchy distribution. Having a shape parameter of 0.5 ensures a flatter distribution, which is more conservative.

This gives:

\[\begin{align*} \tau & \sim \operatorname{Half-Cauchy}(0, 0.5) \ \end{align*}\]

4 Primary Outcome- Dynamic Pain at 24 Hours

The primary outcome for this meta analysis is dynamic pain at 24 hours. This was chosen because dynamic pain is a more reliable outcome compared with static pain. Alternatively, functional pain scores are a useful outcome measure, however, there were no studies with this as an outcome.

4.3 Bivariate Posterior Probability Plot

Compared to a univariate outcome, a multivariate (or bivariate in this case) is advantageous when the outcomes are correlated. In pain studies, a benefit in pain may not be obvious, particularly when alternate analgesia is given (ie no difference in pain scores, but an increase in opioid consumption). Hence, we are interested in modelling the bivariate outcome of dynamic pain scores and opioid consumption. Fitting a Bayesian model allows us to generate conditional probabilities where we may determine:

  • Probability of reduction in pain scores AND a reduction in opioid consumption

  • Probability of reduction in pain scores AND an increase in opioid consumption

  • Probability of an increase in pain scores AND a reduction in opioid consumption

  • Probability of an increase in pain scores AND an increase in opioid consumption.

4.3.1 The Likelihood

The bivariate likelihood is more complex compared to that of the univariate outcome.

\[ \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} Y_{i1} \\ Y_{i2} \end{pmatrix} \sim Normal\biggl\{\begin{pmatrix} \theta_{i1} \\ \theta_{i2} \end{pmatrix}, S_i \biggr\} \tag{Likelihood} \\ \\ S_i=\begin{pmatrix} s^2_{i1} & \rho_{W_{i}}s_{i1}s_{i2}\\ \rho_{W_{i}}s_{i1}s_{i2} & s^2_{i2} \end{pmatrix}\\ \\ \begin{pmatrix} \theta_{i1} \\ \theta_{i2} \end{pmatrix} \sim Normal\biggl\{\begin{pmatrix} \beta_{1} \\ \beta_{2} \end{pmatrix}, D \biggr\} \\ \\ D=\begin{pmatrix} \tau^2_{1} & \rho_{B}\tau_{1}\tau_{2}\\ \rho_{B}\tau_{1}\tau_{2} & \tau^2_{2} \end{pmatrix}\\ \end{align*} \]

where \(Y_{i1}\) and \(Y_{i2}\) are the estimated effects of the underlying true effects (denoted \(\theta_{i1}\) and \(\theta_{i2}\)) , with \(s_{i1}\) and \(s_{i2}\) their associated standard errors. The within-study variance-covariance matrix is given by \(S_{i}\), is and \(D\) is the between study covariance matrix. For a bivariate outcome of mean difference in pain and mean difference in opioid consumption, we assumed a within-study correlation of \(\rho = 0.5\).

4.3.2 Priors

Within a Bayesian framework, the above likelihood is combined with priors of the unknown parameters \(B_{j}\) (the pooled effect of each outcome, including \(B_{1}\) & \(B_{2}\)), \(\tau^2_{j}\) (between-study variance) and \(\rho_{B}\) (between study correlation).

4.4 Sensitivity Analysis with Different Priors

Choosing a prior for a Bayesian analysis is the most subjective component of the analysis. Here, we will perform a sensitivity analysis with different values of priors for \(\mu\) and \(\tau\). The first prior is the informative prior, which is the weakly regularising \({\mu} ∽ N(0, 1)\) and \({\tau} ∽ \operatorname{Half-Cauchy}(0, 0.5)\) prior that we used in the primary analysis.

The second prior is the vague prior, which imparts no information on the posterior estimate, and is given by \({\mu} ∽ N(0, 4)\) and \({\tau} ∽ \operatorname{Half-Cauchy}(0, 4)\).

The third prior is from Turner et al (2015), which examines predictive distributions of for the heterogeneity of variable studies with variable outcomes. For the outcome of ‘pain’, the ‘pharmacological intervention vs placebo/control’ prior is specified by \({\tau} ∽ \operatorname{Log-Normal}(-1.83, 1.52^2)\). For the ‘pharmacological vs pharmacological’ group (for eg truncal blocks vs thoracic epidural), the specified prior is \({\tau} ∽ \operatorname{Log-Normal}(-2.06, 1.58^2)\). For the mean effect, we will use the informative prior, \({\mu} ∽ N(0, 1)\).

As can be seen from the below table, the overall effect is robust to varying priors.

6.0.5 Block Type

Truncal blocks are defined as transversus abdominus plane (TAP), rectus sheath, preperitoneal, paravertebral and erector spinae blocks. Here, we will look at each of these blocks with the different subgroups as above- single shot, placebo continuous infusion, thoracic epidural and wound infusion.

7 Metaregression

7.0.1 Variables for Inclusion in the Metaregression Model

Given that pain is a multidimensional perception that is influenced by a number of factors, it is reasonable to investigate whether the use of truncal blocks varies when these factors are taken into account. For pain, variables such as opioid type (given postoperatively) and analgesic adjuncts (such as paracetamol, NSAIDs and gabapentinoids) can influence pain and pain perception. Other variables that may also influence pain are sex, age and duration of surgery may result in aggregation bias, where there is an assumption that aggregated data also applies to individual data points. Hence, we will not perform a metaregression with those variables.

7.0.2 Priors for the Metaregression Model

The priors will be the same used for the primary analysis

7.0.3 The Meta-Regression Model

Given the aforementioned variables for inclusion into the model and the priors, the meta-regression model is given by the following:

\[ \begin{align*} y_i & \sim Normal(\theta_i, \sigma_i^2) \tag{Likelihood} \\ \theta_i & \sim Normal(\mu, \tau^2)\\ \mu & = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1}Opioid\ Type\ +\beta_{2}Paracetamol\ + \beta_{3}NSAIDs\ + \beta_{4}Gabapentinoids\\ \\ \beta_{i} & \sim \operatorname{Normal}(0, 1) \tag{Priors} \\ \tau & \sim \operatorname{Half-Cauchy}(0, 0.5) \\ \end{align*} \] It should be noted that for the primary meta analysis model, the coefficient of interest was the intercept, which was denoted \(\alpha_{subgroup[k]}\). In this metaregression model, the intercept is given by \(\beta_{0}\) and our coefficient of interest is that of the covariates in the model (ie Opioid Type, Paracetamol, NSAIDs and Gabapentinoids).
